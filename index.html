ImageNet Roulette | Excavating AI | From Apple to Anomaly

My precedent study examines critical projects that reveal the social and political problems embedded in image datasets used to train AI.

ImageNet Roulette, created by Trevor Paglen, was an online tool trained on the “person” category from the ImageNet dataset. It allowed users to upload their photos and see how the system classified them, often producing disturbing results that exposed how biased and offensive labels within a dataset can directly translate into harmful outputs.

In their article “Excavating AI,” Paglen and Kate Crawford expand the critique, highlighting three central issues: harmful labels embedded in datasets; simplistic correlations without context; and exploitative practices like scraping people’s images without consent or compensation. They argue that image classification is never neutral but a deeply political act tied to histories of stereotyping.

These critiques form the core of my study, exposing the risks of current dataset practices. At the same time, I point to possible solutions, such as establishing digital provenance and fair compensation for digital art, and the promise of multimodal simulation AI, which could enable deeper, more contextual understanding and more complex tasks.
